Day 100: Geysir and Gullfoss

Thursday 14 June 2012

Reykjavik, Iceland and points beyond

In one week this journey will be over. Sobering thought.

I’m awake before 0700 and pay too much for a modest breakfast. It’s filling, and that’s enough. I stretch and relax as I wait for my ride. I am picked up on time at 0800, and after a visit to the main office, a full bus departs at 0840.

We pass through some amazing country. A few rolling hills, punctuated with undulating flats and mountains. Much of it is grassy, covered with beautiful blue wildflowers which I’m told were introduced from Alaska, with sheep and stout horses grazing occasionally. Along the narrow road is a large metal pipe leading back to town, coming from up in the hills. The pipes are part of the island’s geothermal energy system, converting natural steam into electricity and using the near-boiling water under the ground to warm homes and heat purified waters for showers. I should add the shower water has a faint sulfurous odor. There are clumps of trees here and there, more imported than indigenous. Our tour guid tells us an old Icelandic joke: what do you do if you get lost in an Icelandic forest? You stand up. It’s apt: most of the trees are pretty short, but anything you can do to protect the thin soil from blowing away.

Around 1000 we stop or Thingvellir, the site of Icelandic government for centuries, from the ninth or tenth century up until a hundred and fifty years ago. Two weeks of every year in June, people would gather from all around, making a festival out of it, no doubt. The chosen leaders would gather and discuss and do the business of governance. There was even one man whose job it was to recite aloud all the laws of the land at these gatherings, taking three years to complete the cycle. Iceland was an oral culture for quite a long time. There were no buildings assembled here. No parliament, no markets or palaces. Just a lovely patch of earth to which they all came. Those were the days.

Fascinating sidebar: as foreign as the Icelandic language sounds, there are moments when it is amazingly familiar, and I am suddenly aware that Icelandic and English have a common ancestor. Many common words.

We move on, continuing through amazing lands, over gorges and oceans of rock. This is the meeting point of tectonic plates which are slowly drifting apart, which goes a long way toward explaining that which makes Iceland so special: it’s confounding geology. Cool air and hot springs, entire fields, not of grass but of thick lichen. Volcanos, and of course, geysers. At 1215 we stop to see Gullfoss, the Golden Falls. Perhaps the most beautiful waterfall I have seen. And by 1400 we are there to see the original Geysir, the source of the word itself. Apparently, the great Geysir is retired now, but he has many brothers and sisters, one blowing about every three or four minutes a good thirty yards into the sky, belching a wall of steam out behind it. Several little geysers gurgle and bubble and steam all over the area. It’s gorgeous.

We continue through the startling landscape, in turns alien then familiar and charming, stopping to see a few sights here and there, even exploring a geothermal power plant. Aside from a profound amount of strong smelling steam and lots of large pipes, there’s really not much to see at a geothermal power plant, but it’s still worthy of a look-see.

We make it back to town before 1800, and I grab a quick bite before heading in to charge my camera back up. Everyone else is planning on a night out, but at ten bucks a beer, I’m just not feeling it. The bar scene seldom appeals to me anyway.

Safe journey.
