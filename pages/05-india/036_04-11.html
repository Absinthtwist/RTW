Day 36: Oh Calcutta

Wednesday 11 April 2012

Kolkata, India

I checked out of Fairlawn and had a good breakfast, then set out for my final day in Kolkata.

The streets are packed with people, vendor stalls and food stands. Hand water pumps every block or so. Shoe-Shines working their trade. Barbers without chair, on the pavement with the rest of us, their customers hunched down for a haircut, or even more dramatic, a straight razor shave, right there on the street. Nobody trip or stumble. Good time to grow out that beard.

There is no McDonalds here. No KFC. There are a few tiny oases of western upscale shopping, but blink and they are gone.

Kolkata isn’t a place of tourist attractions beyond itself. I saw the Victoria Memorial as discussed. A further distance away is a shelter set up by Mother Theresa. There’s the Writers’ Building, mentioned in all the guidebooks. The building itself is nice enough, I just like the idea of an entire building just for writers. The reality, a building for scribes and bureaucrats, is less romantic.

Knowing I will eventually need to cross the Hooghly River, I start heading west, following the flow. I end up in an area with far fewer stalls, with women sorting through greens. I see many people, men and women, with tremendous bundles placed upon their heads walking hurriedly. They seem to all be flowing to an overpass and I figure, what the hell, and I follow. We pass over a rail track and end up in who knows what. A giant flower wreath market. Many many orange flower wreaths, packed with people. At this point, I am without a doubt the only non-Indian around. I just soak it up, try to go with the flow, not offend anyone or stand out more than I already do.

I eventually find my way to the large bridge going over the river, and the difference in tone is dramatic. Paved streets are replaced with crumbling brick. Four and five story buildings are replaced by single story simple brick. Mud, dirt, what sanitation there was to the east has slipped away. I see a cow just hanging out on the street and can only conclude that this is the oft spoken-of Holy Cow. I see another such cow and give a wide berth. The one prominent building here, in bold colors and reasonable repair, and twice as tall as its neighbors, is a Catholic church.

I eventually make my way to the train station, which dwarfs most all of Kolkata I’ve seen to date, including the airport, with the clear exception of the Victoria Memorial. The Writer’s Building was possibly larger as well.But the station, as in China, is a beehive. Full of activity. There are many electric fans for which I am grateful. I grab some local food, delicious, and have a seat on my pack on the floor as I wait for my train.

I’m early and it’s a bit of a wait, but the train does leave on time. I had selected AC second class sleeper, and it’s adequate. On the left side of the train car are two single seats facing each other which shift into a single bed. To the right are two benches, comfortably seating three or four, facing each other, and they too convert to beds. There are likewise beds directly above as well, and this pattern is repeated perhaps a dozen times through the car. I am on the left side with my single bed, and the majority of the AC goes to the other side. I have a curtain for privacy which, when closed, is far too stuffy, so I leave open. I wouldn’t call it comfortable, but it’s enough. I picked up an autobiography by Gandhi in the station, seemed appropriate, read for a bit, the shut out the light. Gonna be a long ride.

Safe journey.
