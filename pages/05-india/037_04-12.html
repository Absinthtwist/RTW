Day 37: The Sacred River Ganga

Tursday 12 April 2012

Transition to Varanasi, India

Almost noon and I’m still on the train. The frequent stops don’t help matters. But I have a window seat with a constantly amazing view, so I don’t complain. Sixteen hours on a train is much more tolerable than on an aircraft.

1430

Made it to hotel, but pretty wiped out. Walked from the train station, not far, five and a half kilometers, and I was doing fine till I stepped into the hotel lobby. Suddenly the sweat was coming off me in sheets. Still reasonably hot here in Varanasi, though not quite as hot as Kolkata, but with only a fraction of the humidity. Best part: when I got to my room I was told they just repaired the shower and that I shouldn’t use it for the next

The streets of Kolkata were interesting, but Varanasi wins. More vibrant, chaotic, noisy, muddy, more animals. For the first time I had a child follow me for a block, tugging and then eventually grabbing a hold of my arm. Hewanted a drink of my water. He looked adequately healthy, and I’m sorry, but I protect myself and my water closely.

I relax for a bit then am out for a bite to eat and to explore. Remiscent of my Thai tattoo (which is doing nicely btw, solo aftercare on a back tattoo is complicated but manageable), one of my goals got this stage of the trip is rather unsanitary: to bathe in the Sacred River Ganges. It is notoriously filthy, but I would regret it and call myself a weenie if I didn’t get in there at least a little. Never mind the sewage or human corpses.

In the restaurant, at a loss for what to order, I see the local specialties combo plate and give it a go. We start with a sort of salad, reminiscent of pico de gallo with cucumbers served over what I swear is a baked tortilla. Indian tostada. I don’t make this stuff up, I just report it. Next is a cup of soup. It’s a mildly sweet tomato soup with a couple of lovely croutons. Quite enjoyable. Then the big one. A huge plate with what I would call nan, a cup of light rice, and several small cups of various creams for dipping. There is also a lassi and a small round of sweet dough infused with syrup. Amazing, but too much food even for me. I do what damage I can, express my gratitude, and am on my way.

Wow.

I walk to the river’s edge, to the ghats. There are certainly more westerners here than I have yet seen in India. Though I must say, any tourist coming here is probably not wearing the Bermuda shorts complaining about no HBO or jacuzzi at their hotel. I think at this point you are less a tourist and more a traveler. I allow a couple kids to talk me into buying two floating candles, a watch a few other kids play a pick-up game of cricket, which as an American I completely fail to understand. I’ve had it explained to me, I understood at the time, then five minutes later was again in total confusion.

As the sun set, I hired a man to take me onto the river in his boat. Really, all you have to do is hold still and someone will come to you asking if you want a boat. It’s just like on the streets, you are constantly saying no thank you to offers of tuk-tuk rides.

My boatman’s name is Ganesa, and he seems quite nice, though I don’t fully understand everything he says (sidebar: I flipped through TV in Kolkata to find Harry Potter, in English with English subtitles. Same language, same trouble understanding one another). The sky slowly darkens as we proceed.I can see that this is really a far more powerful way to see the riverbank, you get a far better look at the amazing buildings. And as dusk turns to evening, people prepare for their big rituals.

For those who do know, it’s worth a pause here. The Hindu people worship the Ganges River as a Goddess. They don’t say the Ganges, they just say Ganga, like the name of a person. No “the” to signify a thing rather than a person. Further, they believe that those who die here, or those whose bodies are cremated and whose ashes are given to Ganga, those people escape the karmic wheel of reincarnation and they go straight to Nirvana. So needless to say, they do a pretty good trade here. Every day, every night, seven days a week, no holiday breaks, there is always always at least one human body being burned to ashes here on the shore of Ganga. This is a very sacred thing. When a man dies, his son brings him here to perform the rights. He shaves his head and wears only white, the Hindu color of mourning. The body is prepared. Special scented wood is gathered and I pyre is built. He takes a bit of straw to the eternal flame, a fire that has been continually tended for the last twenty-six hundred years or so (Varanasi is one of the oldest continually inhabited cities in the world), and the pyre is set ablaze. I must have seen a dozen or more such pyres this evening, and I can tell you, it is a powerful sight.

At one point, my boatman brought me ashore right close to a grouping of five or more pyres. One of the men in charge of these fires walked me around, showed me the wood, the eternal flame, how they prepare the pyres. I was inches away from the fires. One of the most amazing and intense experiences I can remember having. Was I shown all this as a way to get money? No doubt (people keep showing up to be burned even without cash for the wood, etc). Did I care? Not particularly. Did I give money? Not as much as they were hoping, I’m sure, but still, for me, a good amount. And I absolutely feel that I got my money’s worth. Truly fantastic.

After that, my boatman rowed me back. As we drifted away from the main fires and rituals and music (though we did pass a couple of quiet, more modest and somber fires), things became quite peaceful. Quiet. The water under the oars. Chirping of insects. The distant sound of drums carried on the wind.

Hard to top that, though I’m told that dawn is a great experience too. Just not sure I’m gonna want to get to at 0500 after that train ride. Plus I still have the following day. Savor it.

All in all, a good day.

Safe journey.
