Day 35: Welcome To India

Tuesday 10 April 2012

Kolkata, India

0900 Fairlawn Hotel, Kolkata, India

Let myself sleep in as late as I could. Still not entirely certain of my plans today.

I did a quick check online: the amount of rupees I picked up at the currency exchange is about a year’s income for low-middle class people here. So yeah, lots of money. They average about US $2 a day. So the tips I gave to my cab driver and the dude who showed me to my room last night, both insanely good my local standards. I was afraid I was being cheap. Worth paying attention to. And I’ll need to get some smaller bills. The smallest I have is 500 rupees, or just under US $10. So in other words, a rupee here to the locals is what a dollar might be to me in the States.

I’ve had no time to get any bottled water. Brushing my teeth without any water at all, certainly not tap water, was interesting but not s big deal. Reasonable shower. A tea set and electric kettle in the room, so a make myself a cup of tea, boiled water should be ok, can’t fear everything.

Went downstairs and had breakfast. Fried eggs, toast, fried tomato, mushrooms, and a small circle of hash or sausage. And of course, tea. Pot brought to the table, complete with tea- cozy. Tea grounds used, not bags. I had a glance at the dregs of my cup, to see if I could read tea leaves without any success. All I really know to look for I learned from Harry Potter, no grim that I could see.

From there I went for a walk. Visions of trudging through human waste as I step over dead children came to naught. The streets are certainly third world, but none of the insanity one hears tales of.

Of course, it’s early yet. My hotel is one block away from the Indian Museum, which I understand is quite large and interesting, but as this is my first time on the streets of India, I give it a pass and keep walking.

After some interesting rambling, I make it to the rather impressive Victoria Memorial. The grounds surrounding have a pleasant and well maintained park, and the building itself stands out quite distinctly in white marble. I went inside and was surprised to discover a very nice museum, larger than I would have thought possible, complete with a life- size recreation of an old Calcutta street. Many old marble statues of British leaders in India, many beautiful paintings of Indian leaders in India. As the citizen of a country that broke colonial ties with England at the point of a gun but now calls England a dear friend, I find myself curious as to India’s relationship with its British past. Complicated, no doubt. I suppose all imperialism comes with certain advantages, but I cannot doubt that self determination is always always the highest good. I would have taken lots of pics, but the man at the door specifically asked me to take no photographs. A sign I usually obey but not always. A polite and reasonable request: that I must respect.

1330

Indian museum. Good but only one room with AC. Lots of fans but that just doesn’t cut it. Further, I just realized that I have been moving without real rest through this heat and humidity and have had nothing to drink but a little tea this morning. I need to get some water in me. Soon.

1445

Back at Fairlawn. I order chicken tikka and water. Not sure what chicken tikka is, but it’s a start. The chicken tikka is excellent. Thai food is delicious, but nothing compares to the amazingly delicious food of India. I drank a full liter of water with lunch then bought two more liters for later.

240 rupees for lunch, with three liter bottled of water. Not quite five dollars. I can live with that. And the two attractions both were 150r apiece.For foreigners of course, much much cheaper for locals. The two economy thing makes it a little harder for me to get a real sense of costs.

After lunch I return to my room to find the AC running full blast. I know I did not do that, but can’t say I mind just now. I flip through the tv and watch a few moments of local tv, all very dramatic, before taking a wee nap.

I’m up and out soon after, and I walk through the streets past vendors and stalls of every kind. The place is packed with people. Any road that isn’t bustling with cars the pedestrians take over, and when cars come, they move slowly, leaning on their horn as they do.

Interesting side note: on two separate occasions, people whom I would assume are local Indians asked me for directions, one of them started by asking in Hindi! I couldn’t answer his question, but the other question I answered as well as I could. If it was some sort of scam, it certainly seemed without profit. Nothing stolen. Do I look enough like a local? Or at least like an expat? My clothes blend in a little better here than in previous countries, and I declined to carry along my camera. And that makes a big difference, I think: how could I be a tourist without a camera? As it is, my iPhone camera seems to work fine and is far more discreet.

I make it back to my hotel just in time to avoid a thorough soaking from a truly awe inspiring tropical storm. I miss storms like this. I enjoy my dinner, then sit in the common area and read for a bit before eventually turning in.

Tomorrow, more Kolkata during the day, then a sleeper train to my next destination: Varanasi, once known as Benares.

Safe journey.
