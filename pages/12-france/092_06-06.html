Day 92: Caverns Dark And Waterlilies Light

Wednesday 6 June 2012

Paris, France

I am awake and out the door by 0930. I stop and buy more metro tickets to keep me moving, and a pain au chocolate to keep me fat. By 1010, I am stepping out onto the Paris city streets across from the great Paris Opera house. It’s pretty over-the-top, fantastic to look at, if a bit excessive. But hey, if you’re gonna be a bear, be a grizzly, right? As this is a working theatre and they are doing some repairs or some such to the stage, the auditorium is off limits, but the lobby area I have free access to is extraordinary. Neoclassicism was all about looking like the Classical era, only doing it bigger better faster more to prove we’re cooler. Or at least that’s my impression. An impression to link oneself to a previous art movement, providing continuity and justification, but at the same time transcending it. That’s this place.

By 1100 I am back on the street and walking, unhurriedly, I don’t mind if I do less today, toward l’Orangerie Gallery. My camera starts giving me serious trouble and eventually just freezes up. Good thing I have a good camera on my phone.

By 1145 I reach my destination and stroll right in. This really is a rather small gallery, though undoubtedly important. Downstairs has some fantastic works, but the building was created for the works upstairs. Two oval galleries, like a big figure-eight, with profoundly large floor to ceiling canvases covering all but the doors of these two round rooms, surrounding you in a space of color and light: Monet’s masterpieces, his great waterlilies.

The Orangerie doesn’t take long, and by 1245 I’m relaxing on a bench in the Tuileries. I review my options and decide my next stop is the Paris Catacombs. I step through the next metro station entrance and seem to walk forever down staircase after staircase, hallway after hallway. Not quite lost but feeling I should have just walked up on the surface. By 1415 I’m up on the streets if Paris again and feeling cranky, so I know my chocolate croissant breakfast has worn off. In no mood to bother with menus, I go right for the blasphemy: McDonalds. It’s as much a McDonalds as all the others, Paris doesn’t change it that much. Yeah, the Royale with cheese is there, and so too the beer, both of which I decline. I take my time with my crap food, then proceed to the Catacombs, waiting in the long line.

By 1530, I am descending down the long spiral staircase. The Catacomb experience is about a two kilometer walk, and the first is rather mellow. Bare stone walls and low ceilings, lights dim and few and far between, just enough to see by. There are a couple of interesting points where someone carved little castles from the rock, rather charming. Then comes the second kilometer, what most people came for. Darker by far than the first half, and quite damp: at some points the floor was a thin layer of mud over stone, water dripping from the all-too-low ceiling. And of course, the bones. Running out of room, the Parisians of two hundred years ago emptied cemeteries of their bones and neatly stacked them all here. Thousands upon thousands upon thousands of bones lines of skulls stacked upon a wall of femurs. The Cappuccin Monks in Rome were certainly the most decorative with bones, but this place wins overwhelmingly, both for sheer vast numbers and for truly spectacular ambiance. Very creepy, very cool. Walls of bones to either side, the damp dripping air, the low ceiling, all hard to beat.

By 1635 I step out into the light, and walk north. I might make it in time to get into the Pantheon, but I’m not gonna run. Either I make it yes or no. I walk through the Latin Quarter, which really is quite as cool as they say, I can see my hostel over by Gare de Lyon is nowhere near as cool as this place. By 1725 I reach the Pantheon, which is not really religious, despite the name, nor does it look at all like the Panthon in Rome. It’s quite lovely, very Neoclassical in appearance, very much a “look how cool we are” sort of thing. There’s a touch of the “martyrs of the revolution” feel as well, which always strikes a chord with me, but somehow reminds me of Mao’s Mausoleum in Beijing, which kinda kills the patriotic mood for me. I step out and have a seat on the steps, where down the street a couple kilometers, easily visible, stands the Eiffel Tower, which I confess still makes me smile.

I keep walking north, walking through the Latin Quarter, past the Sorbonne, and at 1820 I am back in front of Notre Dame and decide to step in. There is a mass, so I have a seat. The scent of incense wafts through the great room, and the sound of church organ and choral singing. Hard to beat.

And that’s pretty much my day.

Safe journey.
