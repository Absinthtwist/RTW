Day 49: Amman

Tuesday 24 April 2012

Transition from New Delhi, India to Amman, Jordan

My trip to Agra has worn me out, and my day is far from done. Just past midnight when I get in, starting in on Tuesday. I grab a shower and pack up, squeeze in a power-nap, and am riding a taxi to the airport by 0230.

Security, etc has me at my gate just before 0400, and they are boarding shortly thereafter. I am really ready for bed. I nod off a bit on the plane, but nothing I’d really call sleep. I watch an in-flight movie, the most recent mission impossible, which was kinda blah.

Now it’s worth mentioning that, dollar for day, this is by far the most expensive leg of my trip, at about twice the base cost. Why did I do this? Well, I had a short space of time and things I wanted to see, I had an awareness that after India a little pampering might not go amiss, and frankly, as an American in the Middle-East, there was the tiniest bit of nervousness, especially with Arab Spring Politics changing the game. I am not proud of this, but there it is.

That said, I must say Jordan is wonderful so far. I try not to let India blow the grading curb, but regardless, Jordan reminds me why I travel. Clean, charming, the weather is fantastic – San Diego weather. I am greeted by my guide at the airport and we drive to Amman. My guide’s name is Ibrahim, and he is humorous and informative, and his english is quite good. We decide on 0800 for a start tomorrow, enough time for breakfast, but still early.

I am led to my room, which is cozy and comfortable, and I open my curtains for the noon view of Amman, just in time to hear the call to prayer start. Isuppose there are some Americans that might feel uncomfortable hearing that sound, but I find it very beautiful, very haunting, and an excellent welcome. There’s no denying that India threw me off my game, good to back in my comfort zone. The thought that the Middle East made me nervous just sounds embarrassing now.

My body demands a nap, but I keep it at ninety minutes lest I stay awake all night and hate tomorrow. After feeling groggy and useless for thirty minutes, I head out to see a bit of the city.

Even without the benchmark of India, Jordan is fantastic. People are friendly and helpful, the streets are reasonably clean, and the music is catchy. Many women do cover their hair, though this is not obligatory and many other women do not. A few women cover everything but their eyes, others wear short sleeves and skirts, indistinguishable from the west. Of the many women who do choose to cover their hair, I am impressed by how alluring they can be: beautiful figures well accented, gorgeous faces. I am struck with the notion that, when possible, women will continue to find ways to be beautiful. One woman’s obstacle is another woman’s challenge. Needless to say, there are some disturbingly extreme cases out there, but nothing like that is apparent here. As if to underline a point I’m still digesting, I walk through a popular mall and can very clearly see that Amman has a thriving lingerie trade. Gotta say, that knowledge puts a new spin on my thoughts on the hijab.

While in the mall, I do my one adventurous deed for the day and get my hair cut. My stylist spoke passable English, and it went alright, but I can’t say I exactly look my sexiest. Oh well. At least it’s not hanging in my eyes any more.

I make the walk home, wash some clothes and take it easy. At dusk I once again hear the call to prayer, and once again I am struck by its beauty. I have left the far east behind and entered a different world.

Safe journey.
