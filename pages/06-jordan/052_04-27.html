Day 52: Tea With Bedouins

Friday 27 April 2012

Wadi Rum, Jordan

Up and out the door by 0800, saying goodbye to the Petra Mountains. We head south and downward, into true desert, into Wadi Rum. Very little of the ocean-like forty foot sand dunes here (those are more into the Arabian peninsula and north African Sahara), but still vast open space of fine red sand, almost like dust, the emptiness broken by giant mountains of rock. I am reminded again that all of this was once at the bottom of an ocean, and that is very easy to imagine.

We make it to the village at the edge of Wadi Rum, where the paved road abruptly ends, and I trade guides for a few hours. My usual driver shall remain behind with his comfortable car, and I get into a Toyota 4×4, accompanied by my Bedouin driver. We depart the village and the road and drive through the empty sand, great mountains of rock to either side in the distance.

Our first stop is a small well, named either from the Hollywood movie or the actual person Lawrence of Arabia. Limits in my guide’s English prevent me from clearly understand after whom the well was named, but I let it go. I do learn, however, that my guide’s father worked on the 1962 movie, and that his grandfather fought and died in the WWI campaign under Lawrence. Given my guide’s age, I wonder if language has him skipping a generation, but I tend to think, under the circumstances, that he is being truthful, even if he actually meant grandfather and great-grandfather. The well itself is a bit of a climb up the rock face, then piped down to sand level.

After looking around and trying to take it all in, I find my guide relaxing in a near by Bedouin tent. It’s a large tent, with a full side open to air, and a floor covered in beautiful carpets. In a neat row against the back are cushions, like sofa cushions, but on the floor. Every six feet or so in line with the cushions is a camel saddle, or something put together to look like one but more comfortable. Others are relaxed in the tent, some seated cross-legged, others leaning against saddles, and still others just lounging comfortably on their sides. I am welcomed and invited in, and offered tea or coffee. I take a seat and ask for the tea. The tea comes in a small glass, and is delicious. I am told that they prepare the tea with a mixture of sage and cinnamon and cardamom, and for two dinars I buy a bag of the stuff, only afterward imaging trying to explain to customs that it’s really just sage. Meh, two dinars. It’s like three bucks. The tea itself was free, and the experience and the hospitality are beyond price.

My driver and I move on to our next goal, off-roading over sand dunes with glee. We come to a massive rock rising from the sand, reminding me briefly of Uluru, the great Ayers Rock in central Australia. At the center of this giant rock is a small fissure, and I go in alone to explore. After climbing through a bit, I make the most amazing discovery, no doubt the reason my guide brought me here: carvings on the wall, dating from who knows when. Some in Arabic, so that could be recent, but other bits, pictographic, looks much older. It’s magical.

Once completed, I hike back and join my guide in another, larger tent, and another delicious cup of tea. Enjoying the experience, I allow myself to be talked into buying the traditional red and white head scarf of the Jordanian man. Someone helps me tie it on well, and I am ready to slip back out into the desert. My dark complexion, not for the first time, has many locals saying I could easily pass among them. I take it as a compliment and head back out into the desert.

Next my guide takes me to a lovely bit of rock looking out into the endless desert. Erosion has worn a lovely window arch into the rock, and if I’m not mistaken, this very arch was used to great effect in a very famous shot in David Lean’s film (what else could it be?), Lawrence of Arabia. Naturally, I stop for a few pictures, then move on.

After three amazing hours in the desert with my guide, we return to the village and I switch both car and driver, and make the long voyage back to Amman. It’s about a four hour drive, and I try to get a little rest. Upon return to the city, my wonderful guide, Ibrahim, takes me, at my request, to a place to get good shawarma, a food I’ve come to love back in the states, though honestly, as good as it is, lacks a certain something it gains in the export. I still gobble it down with joy, but I confess I’ve had better.

That’s about it. Tomorrow I actually get to sleep in a bit, then to the airport, and to Egypt. I’ve kept my ear to the ground and feel its still safe. Like anywhere else: just don’t be stupid and don’t be a dick. I make it an easy night, do a little laundry, and get some much needed rest.

Safe journey!
