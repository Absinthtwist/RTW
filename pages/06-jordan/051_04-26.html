Day 51: Petra

Thursday 26 April 2012

Amman, Jordan with side trip to Petra

I make an early start with an 0530 wake up call, have a rushed shower and breakfast, and we are out the door by 0615. Mission: Petra. Plan: get there before the masses overtake the place.

This city exists pretty much just for Petra, so it’s a five minute drive to the gate. From here it’s a bit of a walk, but a pleasant one. There are brief moments of the signature Petra carving, but not a great deal yet. We come to what is known as the Siq, I’d guess just under a kilometer through a narrow fissure in the rocks, the walls towering up on either side. At narrowest, perhaps fifteen feet, thirty feet at the widest. I can already see that the magic is not just in the famous carvings, but in the beauty of the rocks themselves. Breathtaking sandstone rippling with reds and yellows, with veins of black and hints of blues and greens. Once the bottom of an ocean, this place is a study in erosion: water, wind, and chisel.

We round a corner and the fissure opens to reveal the most iconic image of Petra: the Treasury. Truly, it is beautiful, reaching fifty feet or more. Now I must make something very clear here: for as much as the media ever shows, you would think that this one building is Petra. Far from it, Petra was once a large city, home to fifty-thousand. They certainly didn’t all climb into that one structure.

The people who created all this were the Nabateans, caravan merchants quite good with water management and a chisel, they created an oasis in the desert which served as a commercial hub for centuries. This began, as best I can tell, around 200 BC, and continued prosperously, being folded into the Roman Empire around 100 AD. The Romans built fine roads connecting Petra to other major cities in the region, but the new Roman presence was to prove ill for the Nabateans. As the Romans put more energy into the region, other trade routes took precedent, and Petra faded away, to be rediscovered (at least as far as Europeans were concerned) in the 1800s. The style of the carved facades in Petra was very clearly influenced by Greek and Egyptian art, and later by the Romans. Where the Greeks carved marble from a quarry and stacked it up to make a temple, the Nabateans would carve that same temple, or at least it’s front, from the rock, then carve out a reasonably large chamber inside.

As for the so-called Treasury, the locals became convinced that that first impressive structure they saw had gold hidden away within it somewhere. There are still bullet holes where they tried to somehow free the gold. The name Treasury stuck, though the building itself was actually a temple.

In total I spent about ten hours hiking through the truly vast site, up stone carved steps when possible, climbing when not. I don’t mind saying I wore myself out but good. Some of the best sights were high up and distant, but always worth it. Thoroughly exhausted and covered in dust, I returned to the entrance for my ride back to the hotel.

Quite worn out, I still had one adventure left for the day. Though in retrospect it seems foolish, but I took a quick shower before heading off for my first experience in a Turkish Bath. I told the man there I was new at this, and he kindly guided me through the process. I should also mention that I fully intended to do this while in Istanbul, and thought of this as a sort of trial run, so I had nothing to lose.

I locked up my belongings in a small locker provided, taking the small key around my wrist. I have a towel wrapped about my waist, and I’ve kept my underwear on, though I think this was a mistake and I won’t repeat that next time. I am led to my first sauna experience, which was quite intense. At first it felt like a macho dare: how much heat can you take, but I let myself relax into it and it became enjoyable. Then I cooled off in an adjoining steam room, though no where near as hot. I am next led to a room where I am rinsed off with bowlfulls of pleasantly hot water. Next my skin is scrubbed by an abrasive cloth. I am rinsed again, then led to yet another room. I lay on a table and am lathered up and scrubbed. What comes next can charitably be called a massage, but also might be called brutality. He shoves into my muscles in some pretty painful ways. I am again rinsed off, then given fresh towels, and led to yet another room to sit and sip a pleasant tea. Painful, but wonderful. Feeling like rubber, I return to my room and am ready for a deep sleep.

Safe journey.
