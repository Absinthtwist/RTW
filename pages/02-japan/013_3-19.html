Day 13: Nara

Nara, Japan

Today I go to Nara. Nara is about an hour’s train ride from Kyoto, not too bad. And from there perhaps a mile walk to Nara Park. There’s a local myth of some God coming in riding a deer, so as a result deer are considered messengers of the godsand protected. There must be hundreds of deer just hanging out through the very large park.

Vendors sell biscuits for tourists to pass around to the deer, and the deer are most insistent about wanting to be fed. They are completely unafraid of humans, seem to see us as food dispensers, and they walk right up to you. They have no problem with you walking up and touching them, petting them, whatever, so long as there is food involved. And they don’t mind following you, nudging you, bullying you for the food they know is coming. Thankfully, all their antlers have been filed down to a nub. Again, hundreds of deer. Crazy.

There are a great many temples here in Nara, including one of the tallest pagodas in Japan, but the most noteworthy item is the giant Buddha. One of the largest Buddha statues anywhere, and the centerpiece for a magnificent temple. For my money, this was more beautiful than anything in China, including the Forbidden City. There were also a couple of small but gorgeous museums in Nara Park, beautifully done. Sorry, no photography allowed in either. Once I’d had my fill of these beautiful temples and hyper-aggressive deer (one kept biting at my pants to express himself and his desire for my food), it was back on the train and back to my neighborhood. I stopped to grab some food, and again, no idea what the name of the place was, but it was good. I think it was some sort of Mongolian Grill. The dish I ate was called a Gengis Kahn. I was seated at a bar/counter, which was all there was of this place. And before be below the counter was what looked like a crock pot, but was actually a small wood stove.

Loaded with fuel and covered with a convex grilling surface, a square inch church of animal fat was placed at top center and allowed to grease the cooking surface. Then the food was added: onions, peppers, pumpkin and carrots, tons of bean sprouts, and chunks of lamb meat. I was given a bowl of brown liquid, it reminded me in appearance to Chinese hoisin peanut sauce, but no peanut flavor.

Once the food was adequately grilled, you would use chopsticks to grab a bit, dip it in the brown sauce then eat. Quite good, actually. Every now and then the waitress would come over with her tongs and redistribute the foodto keep it warm evenly and closer to me to grab. Washed down with a Japanese beer, all around enjoyable. In the morning, I’m off to Tokyo! Safe journey.
