Day 28: So This Is What Heaven Looks Like…

Tuesday 3 April 2012

Ayutthaya, Thailand

Awoke before 0700 then out to rake and shovel away elephant pens and make them nice. I don’t mind saying it was some hard work on my soft hands. More riding, for longer distances. Not exactly relaxed or confident,but feeling much improved in my riding. All in all a good day, not dissimilar to yesterday.

My fellow guests here are composed of two more yanks like myself, these two from Texas, an Australian couple, a mother- daughter pair from Canada, and two more from the UK. A good mix. The staff running the elephant stay run similar lines, Australian and British. The mahouts have a village adjoining the area where we are, the elephant kraal.

The best translation to kraal I can see would be royal stables. For centuries elephants were the superpower here. For industries like lumber (all old wood logging is now banned here), and even more: for war. Imagine a fully armored elephant charging you: screwed. Working with and riding these amazing animals, you really get a sense of their power, their toughness. At first the mahouts seemed to hit them too hard, but even affectionate pats need to come pretty hard through that skin. These aren’t big horses with trunks. An elephant is more like a Sherman tank.

Solid. Pushing a d pulling a d tugging and hitting mean almost nothing to them. And even the babies are easily strong enough to end me. They are very smart, but still smart animals, and do well with boundaries and guidance.

I have also been told that circus elephants typically outlive zoo elephants, for the very obvious reasons of purpose and stimulation. A well cared for elephant can live into her eighties. Here at this kraal there are well over a hundred elephants, many of them rescues, but many also from breeding. I am told that they have had nearly fifty successful deliveries, and we are shown an elephant ready to deliver any time.

Other rescues have appeared here. Several dogs, a couple cats, including one with feline AIDS in seclusion, a macaque injured in the wild now living well with no legs and one arm, and a crocodilefish in a tank of water. Quite a collection.

On the outside wall of the hut I am in is a soberingly high water mark. Just three months ago this area was devastated by floods. It’s an even more dramatic sight at the mahout village,

A hut from the Mohout village. That brown line up there is the water line from the recent flood where much of the damage has yet to be repaired. There’s talk of putting the whole village on stilts, but cost aside, that’s quite a time consuming venture.

Well, after a long day we all lounge together over drinks and talk about everything and nothing, our bellies full of delicious Thai food. Tomorrow starts early, so in ones and twos we all drift to bed.

Safe journey.
