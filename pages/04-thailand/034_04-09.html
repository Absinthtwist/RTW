Day 34: Easy Day and Flight to Kolkata

Monday 9 April 2012

transition from Bangkok, Thailand to Kolkata, India

1045

Back to Siam mall. These travel days are hard for me. I don’t want to do anything too heavy or time consuming, don’t want to take even a minor risk of missing a flight. Also, I just want to go go. I love Thailand, but now it’s time for India. So I will chill. I picked up a device which I believe will let me charge my iPhone using AA batteries. If it works, it might be a game changer. I’m trying to decide on seeing another movie or seeing the aquarium here. As a rule, aquariums can only hold my interest a couple hours, and it’s about five times more expensive. They also have a Madame Tussaud’s, but that is a bridge too far.

I went ahead and watched Titanic. The 3D was a total waste. All it seemed to do was make the screen dark. I’ve heard this before, but this is the first time I’ve really gotten the before-after with a film I have seen many times and know well. More than once I caught myself trying to take off my sunglasses only to realize they were the 3D glasses.

1920

Airport. Boarding now. Security screenings very serious here. Two screenings. And they stopped two things all the other airports let by: my tiny collapsible sewing scissors and a length of cord I kept for drying clothes or just the unexpected. Certainly don’t want me garroting anyone. Anyway, it helps lighten the bag. The first screening is the normal one, the second, at the gate before you board, included a hand search of every bag and a metal detecting wand. I try not to be annoyed by it. Thailand and India have both had problems with terrorism, far be it from me to bitch.

Watched film The Debt on the flight. Pretty good. I respect the ending, but it still felt a little flat.

A first: the immigration gave me what felt was a warm and genuine welcome to his country. Wonderful start.

And again with the bag screening as you enter the airport. Kolkata airport is not impressive, just saying. Bangkok was big and modern and shiny. This one is very tiny and looks old and neglected. Less an international airport and more a barnstop. Siem Reap Airport was more impressive. No cash machine in sight, so I cash in some of my backup money. I budgeted around $1300 for my sixteen days in India, knowing it was a very conservative figure. When I exchange $800, the 38,000 rupees he gives me is such a huge stack that I am suddenly a little nervous. I look like a drug dealer with this stack. Or a very easy mark. So either I’m going to be doing very well indeed with my budget, or every transaction will take a zillion bills. I’m thinking the former.

2240

Indian taxi. I want to laugh but repress it. All the stories, every cliche is true. Crazy traffic. Honk for everything. Coming up behind a car? Honk a bunch. Changing lanes (did I say lanes? I meant vague lane-ish regions of the road)? Honk. Cracks me up. The cab itself looks like it could fall apart any second, and looks nearly as old as me. All the cabs are pretty much the same. Want air conditioning? Roll down a window. On the cab dash are tiny plastic altars with tiny pink and green lights.

On the drive in, I saw many areas on the pavement laid out with blankets and people sleeping on them. Many many. Standard American homelessness seeks to be subtle. Not these. It just seems normal, accepted. They don’t look destitute, just… sleeping outside. And why not, it’s a pleasant night.

By 2330 I arrive at the Fairlawn Hotel. It feels like something out of a Merchant-Ivory film. It has age to it. I read that this hotel has been family owned since the 1930s, but the place seems older than that. And intensely British. I step into this place and suddenly feel the urge for a gin and tonic. I am led up to my room, an entire room all to myself, complete with AC, ceiling fan, and my own bathroom with an old style bathtub (alas no claw feet on the tub, that would have been too perfect).

Tomorrow I go explore Kolkata. Not entirely sure what my plan is. Find the station for tomorrow’s train. Really, that’s about it.

Safe journey.
