Day 27: Elephants!

Monday 2 April 2012

Ayutthaya, Thailand

ElephantStay

0745

0745 journey already starting. My ride to the elephant stay sanctuary was scheduled for 0800, and at 0740 I went to the desk of my hostel to check out and relax a moment, maybe score some breakfast, only to discover the driver is ready to go. Should be 60-90 minutes.

Arrived by 0910. Big welcome get-together with staff, volunteers and the other ten or so guests. Safety, protocol, what have you. Soon enough we are each assigned an elephant of our own for the duration of our stay, my elephant is named “Honey.”

Before much longer we are up and meeting our elephants. And right to the point, we kick off our shoes and up a staircase then sit, bareback, on the neck of the elephant, just forward of her massive shoulders. This is our first encounter with the amazing Thai elephant handlers, called mahouts (pronounced ma-hoot). To the mahout, this is not a job, this is culture, this is tradition, this is family. The mahout bonds with his or her elephant at birth, and the elephant is deeply loved. And not just by the mahouts: Thaiculture at large holds the elephant in high esteem. In the Buddhist karmic cycle of reincarnation, the elephant is the step before being reborn human.

So I awkwardly find my position on my elephant’s large neck. My mahout is right behind me, helping me. Everything you may have learned from riding horses is completely useless here. Your bare feet dangle around your elephant’s ears, and her shoulders roll when she walks. You end up with your hands forward, bracing yourself on the top of her head while you learn to just roll your hips as she goes. Once you get the feel of it, your hands are really only for balance. We don’t go far, perhaps a five minute slow walk to the river, where all ten elephants walk into the water, and they don’t mind dunking their heads. As they roll down, it feels a bit like what I imagine a bucking bronco must feel like, only in slow motion. Water up to my armpits, and don’t forget, elephants like to spray. Another word of warning: elephant poo floats, that’s all I’m saying.

We ride the elephants back to our staircase and one by one disembark. The rest of the day is spent hauling around pineapple tops and mangos to feed to our elephants, and scrubbing our elephants down (avid getting very wet ourselves in the process). Around 1600 we had a second, longer ride, then led our girls out to their sleep area.

After that, we all share a a few beers and indulge in extraordinary Thai food, made by a very lovely woman of little English. They reduce the spiciness for us western wussies, otherwise it is to die for.

From there I return to my air- conditioned hut and I sleep like a rock.

Safe journey!
