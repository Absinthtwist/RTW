Day 55: Luxor Temple

Monday 30 April 2012

Luxor, Egypt

Slept in a bit today, and took my time with breakfast, chatting casually with my fellow travelers. It appears we will all be departing on the same train, all bound for Cairo tomorrow night. All the same, even taking my time, I am out the door by 0830.

Rather than a driver, I return to my area of greatest comfort: solo foot travel. It’s perhaps a kilometer at most to the ferry terminal. It is, of course, surrounded by many boats and locals, all of whom want to distract me from the vastly cheaper ferry. I ignore them and am across the Nile in minutes. Right off the ferry just across the street lies the beautiful Luxor Temple. I walk unhurriedly around the perimeter, enjoying the view while casually looking for an entrance. I find a staircase going up and I take it, only to discover a small but beautiful mosque, built within and upon the Egyptian Temple. As I prepare to politely back away, a man in traditional Arab- Egyptian dress comes forward and greets me. He introduces himself as the imam of this mosque and invites me in. I get over the baksheesh thing in my head and remember that experiences like this are exactly why I have come, a little baksheesh is worth it.

He leads me in to the small mosque, which he tells me is eight hundred years old. The pharaonic columns of the Luxor Temple have been folded into the mosque, the ancient Egyptian art flowing beautifully into the Islamic. There is an area around the main building, covered with rugs, where a couple people are engaged in conversation, another two men are napping. The imam leads me into the main chamber, the prayer room,again with the pharaonic art mixing with Islam. I have a lovely if limited conversation with the imam where we agree on harmony and love between all peoples, and I slip him a generous tip and respectfully move on.

The entrance to Luxor Temple is just a short way further, and I enter the grounds. To the right is what looks to have once been the grand entryway, a hundred yard path of stones lined on both sides by rows of sphinxes. To the left, aligned with the sphinx path, is the impressive entrance to the temple proper.

It’s a large complex as these things go, about the size of a large cathedral. In my head I remind myself that Karnak Temple, which I shall see tomorrow, is at least three times larger. Two great statues sit in symmetrical placement of the doorway, with one towering obelisk to the left of the door. It is safe to assume that once upon a time a second obelisk stood in balance, but it is long gone. A quick check on wikipedia tells me that the missing obelisk is, in fact, the centerpiece of the Place de la Concorde in Paris. Guess they had to get it from somewhere.

On the walls throughout all the temple are the classical images of hieroglyphs, cartouches, and the fabulous figures of Pharaohs, gods, and mortal men and women about their lives. Great tremendous columns in rows rise up to support nothing but themselves. I cannot say what they once supported, the distance between many of them seeming far too great to support anything from that era. I can see, perhaps twenty or more feet above the floor of the temple, the mosque I do recently explored. And further down in the temple I find columns and an archway that I would bet by backpack on were not from the Ancient Egyptian Pharaonic period. And the plaster, still visible on the upper portions of the tall walls, spread over and concealing the ancient carved reliefs, still bears hints of fresco images from a far later period, looking distinctly Roman or possibly even Christian to my eyes.

After Luxor Temple, everything else is just a lite, so I go for a walk. I explore the streets of the city, walking through a souk or market, I pass through stalls of cheap Egyptian knock-offs and tee shirts, past great long rows of fresh fruits and vegetables, all looking a bit weak in my eyes. The vegetables in China were amazing, these all look a bit sad. I pass a shoe shine, seated at the curb, waiting for his next customer, passing the time sucking on a hookah. a great large fabulous looking building under construction, amosque or other religious structure, the two towers beside the great dome looking far more Coptic than Islamic to me.

After drifting through the streets and hot sun (and a stop for a cool drink), I make my way to the Mummification Museum. Small and specialized, very well done. I enjoyed this museum very much, and was done all too soon. Upon returning to the street, I see the wind is now in full force, with dust filling the air. I take the ferry back and return to my room to hide from the heat and relax for the rest of the day. Tomorrow I shall see Karnak Temple, and in the evening depart for Cairo. I’d say I feel ready to leave Luxor and move on.

Safe journey.
