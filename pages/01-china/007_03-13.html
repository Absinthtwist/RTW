Day Seven

Tuesday 13 March 2012

Beijing, China

Train pulled in around 0830. I considered walking the 4 miles to my hostel, but the 25f weather changed my mind and I took a bus. The hostel itself has some lovely atmosphere and looks to be in a fun neighborhood, I’ll explore soon. Once again recharging batteries. Tempted to say to hell with my schedule and go to see the Forbidden City now, but without camera batteries seems kinda stupid.

Well, after a little downtime, and with only my phone for a camera (until batteries charge up), I could wait no longer and set out. I decided that I would not go into the Forbidden City without my camera, a decision I wisely adhered to. I wandered through an amazing labyrinth of markets and food vendors, becoming more and more upscale, until I came to Qianmen Dajie, called the “Emperor’s Road.” It had been cleared out by the revolution then recently refurbished (read: rebuilt from scratch) in preparation for the Olympics. It’s a lovely open-air mall with a beautiful Chinese flair, but with unmistakable western influences. Many big name western shops here, but also many more Chinese.

At the northern end of this avenue is an amazing structure that looks as though it a great gate to a wall that no longer exists. I don’t know about the wall, but we are very close to the Forbidden City. There are two of these gigantic gate structures, aligned with one another, and in a direct line to theForbidden City with Tiananmen Square in between, so it seems possible. The street between me and the Square is quite a big one, and the crossing is not over the street but under it. So through the underpass I go, through a security screening, my daypack through the X-ray machine. The security are patting down locals, but don’t seem terribly concerned with western tourists. Among items blocked by security are cigarette lighters, and at this and other security point there is a large plastic tub full of lighters. People exit the secure zone and just grab one.

Into Tiananmen Square. It’s pretty big. I seem to remember reading somewhere that its the largest such square, beating out the Place de Concorde in Paris. Much of the southern portion is taken up by a huge building which is the final testing place of Chairman Mao. As I understand it, he explicitly stated his wish to be cremated. But there he is, his preserved remains in a glass coffin on display. Alas, the Chairman was closed for renovation and I did not have the pleasure.

As I walked through the Square, no less than three conspicuously attractive Chinese ladies came up to me wanting to practice their English. I have read all about this on the web. They invite you to coffee or a drink – which I politely declined – then either drug you and strip you down like a wrecked car or they buy drinks and the bill comes up for hundreds of dollars and the guy bringing the bill is huge and ready for trouble. I would like to not be cynical. I would like to make new friends and have an amazing connection with a stranger, or even a dashingly romantic whirlwind romance with an exotic young lady. But I don’t wish to be robbed. Or worse. Images of waking in a bathtub full of ice missing a kidney come to mind. I don’t want to be too cautious. Just cautious enough.

In the northern portion of the Square is a huge pillar, it has the look of honoring the martyrs of the Republic or the people or something like that, but I have no idea. To the west is a building I believe is the political body, legislature or the like. To the east is the Chinese Museum of History, which I make a note to check out. To the south is the avenue from which I came, and to the north is the Forbidden City, which is too important to see without a good camera.

From here I drift east toward the museum, and meet two more ladies wanting to practice their English. Must be a girls school they all come from. No guys, no clumps of people. It must be noted, however, that I am noteworthy. Well over six feet tall, many eyes scan me. I’ve just gotten used to it. And one one occasion, a young woman with her friend, with no English at all, was all too excited to see me and insisted on having herpicture taken with me. I was glad to comply, and they were off before I could take a pic of my own.

The Chinese Museum of History is huge. Airport huge. 60 foot high ceilings huge. Made to impress, clearly. The content was fantastic, and easy to understand, with English text in signs. The display areas were easy to follow, but getting from exhibit to exhibit was confusing and over-long. And really, for all that vast scale, there could have been more substance. A good museum, but more worried about making an impression than it has need to be. I was there till they closed, about two hours, and saw perhaps a quarter of their display material. The stuff I most wanted to see, the Ming dynasty and the clash with western powers and the revolution, that timeframe, I got barely a glimpse. Alas, perhaps on another occasion.

Deciding on an early evening a d focusing on charging my batteries, literally and metaphorically, I called it a night and read in the hostel common room as Chinese tv played. The sports program was of no particular interest to me, ice scaring, then later, martial arts, but the commercials kept pulling my attention. The details were obviously Chinese, but what struck me was just how similar these commercials were to American tv. Anyway, that’s all for now. More adventures tomorrow. Safe journey.
